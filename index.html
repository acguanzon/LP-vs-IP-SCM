Abstract

Research Overview

A comprehensive study comparing two classic optimization paradigms applied to a real-world supply chain logistics dataset.

📋 Abstract

This study investigates the performance of Linear Programming (LP) and Integer Programming (IP) in minimizing total transportation cost across a multi-plant, multi-destination supply chain network.

Using a real logistics dataset — containing order lists, freight rates, warehouse capacities, and plant-to-port mappings — we preprocess the data, apply an 80/20 training–testing split, formulate both LP and IP models, and compare their optimal costs and solution times.

The central question is: Does integer-constrained optimization yield meaningfully different costs and performance characteristics compared to its relaxed continuous counterpart in a supply chain context?

🎯 Research Objectives

  • Preprocess and aggregate real supply chain order data by destination demand and plant capacity.
  • Map plant codes to origin ports and compute average freight rates per route.
  • Apply a stratified 80/20 random train–test split for model training and evaluation.
  • Formulate a Transportation Problem as both a Continuous LP and an Integer LP (ILP).
  • Solve both models using the CBC open-source MILP solver via the PuLP library.
  • Compare solution cost, solve time, and optimality between LP and IP models.
  • Identify practical trade-offs between solution quality and computational efficiency.
Methodology

6-Step Preprocessing Pipeline

From raw Excel data to a fully formulated optimization model — a systematic, reproducible pipeline.

STEP 01

Aggregate Demand by Destination

All individual order unit quantities are summed per unique destination port to produce total demand at each node j.

Dj = Σ (unit_qtyk)
   ∀ orders k → destination j
STEP 02

Map Plants to Origin Ports

A lookup dictionary is built from the PlantPorts sheet, mapping each plant code i to its corresponding origin port for freight rate lookups.

OriginPort(i) = Port
   where PlantCode = i
STEP 03

Compute Average Freight Rates

Multiple rate records exist per route. We average all rates for the same origin–destination pair to get a single cost coefficient Cij.

Cij = (1/N) · Σ ratek
   orig=OriginPort(i), dest=j
STEP 04

Load Plant / Warehouse Capacities

Daily capacity Si is loaded for each plant. This forms the supply-side constraint ensuring no plant ships beyond its operational limit.

Supply constraint:
Σj xij ≤ Si   ∀ plant i
STEP 05

Build Decision Variable Index Sets

The full set of decision variables xij is constructed for every plant–destination pair, forming the route-space of the transportation model.

xij ≥ 0   (LP) or
xij ∈ ℤ+ (IP)
i ∈ Plants, j ∈ Destinations
STEP 06

80 / 20 Train–Test Split

Orders are randomly shuffled (seed=42) and split 80/20. The model is trained on the training demand; the test set is held out for evaluation reference.

Train: 0.80 × N orders
Test:  0.20 × N orders
seed = 42 (reproducible)

Formal Optimization Formulation

The transportation problem expressed as both LP and ILP with shared objective and constraints.

🧮 Objective Function Minimize

Minimize Total Freight Cost
min  Z = Σi∈I Σj∈J  Cij · xij
Subject To — Supply Constraints
Σj∈J xij  ≤  Si   ∀ i ∈ Plants
Subject To — Demand Constraints
Σi∈I xij  ≥  Dj   ∀ j ∈ Destinations
Non-negativity / Integrality
xij ≥ 0   (LP — continuous)
xij ∈ ℤ+  (IP — integer)

Findings & Conclusion

Key Takeaways

What the comparison reveals about LP and IP in supply chain optimization.

Discussion

In the classic transportation problem, LP and IP often produce very similar or identical optimal costs. This is because the LP relaxation of a transportation problem with integer supply and demand values inherently has an integral optimal solution — a consequence of the totally unimodular structure of the constraint matrix.

However, in real datasets where capacities and demands are non-integer or have fractional route costs, small gaps between LP and IP costs may emerge. More importantly, solve time differences become significant: LP solves in milliseconds while IP invokes Branch & Bound, which can be orders of magnitude slower for large instances.

In practice, if physical shipment quantities must be whole units, IP is necessary for operational validity, even if the cost increase is negligible. LP remains valuable as a fast lower-bound estimator and in planning contexts where fractional allocation is acceptable.

🏆
Verdict: LP offers faster computation with a globally optimal continuous solution, making it ideal for large-scale planning. IP provides operationally feasible whole-unit allocations at a modest computational cost — the preferred choice when discrete shipments are required. In this dataset, costs are nearly equivalent, with LP providing a tight lower bound.
